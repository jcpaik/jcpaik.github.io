1.1 The Problem

We first define the problem rigorously and introduce essential concepts for the proof.

Assume a fixed plane \(\mathbb {R}^2\) with the \(xy\)-coordinate system, so the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinate each refers to the first (horizontal) and second (vertical) coordinate respectively. First we set up the hallway. [A figure of hallway will help a lot]

Definition 1.1

The hallway \(L = I_H \cup I_V\) is the union of its horizontal side \(I_H = (-\infty , 1] \times [0, 1]\) and vertical side \(I_V = [0, 1] \times (-\infty , 1]\).

Define the horizontal strip \(H = \mathbb {R}\times [0, 1]\) and vertical strip \(V = [0, 1] \times \mathbb {R}\). With this, \(I_H = L \cap H\) and \(I_V = L \cap V\).

We want to move the sofa from the hallway’s horizontal side \(I_H\) to its vertical side \(I_V\). We also give names to the sides and vertices of the hallway \(L\) for future use. The convention is that the sides are in lowercase and the vertices are in boldcase lowercase. The names of sides follow that of [ Rom18 ] .
Definition 1.2

Let \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) and \(d\) be the sides

\begin{align*} a & = \{ 1\} \times (-\infty , 1] \qquad b = \{ 0\} \times (-\infty , 0] \\ c & = (-\infty , 1] \times \{ 1\} \qquad d = (-\infty , 0] \times \{ 0\} \end{align*}

of \(L\).

Definition 1.3

Let \(\mathbf{x}= (0, 0)\) and \(\mathbf{y}= (1, 1)\) be the inner and outer corner of the hallway \(L\) respectively.

We will describe the movement by a continuum of orientation-preserving isometries of \(\mathbb {R}^2\). First, we use the following notations for translation and rotation maps.

Definition 1.4

For any vector \(v \in \mathbb {R}^2\), let \(T_v\) be the translation map \(T_v(w) = w + v\) of the plane by \(v\). For any \(v \in \mathbb {R}^2\) and \(C \subset \mathbb {R}^2\), write \(T_v(C)\) as \(C + v\). For any angle \(\theta \in \mathbb {R}/ 2 \pi \mathbb {Z}\), let \(R_\theta \) be the rotation map of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) along the center of the plane with a counterclockwise angle \(\theta \).

We adopt the following equivalent definition of an orientation-preserving isometry of \(\mathbb {R}^2\). For the proof of equivalence to a usual definition, see [Insert a suitable geometry book here].
Definition 1.5

A map \(f \colon \mathbb {R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb {R}^2\) is called a orientation-preserving isometry of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) if it is the composition \(T_v \circ R_\theta \) of a rotation followed by a translation.

The following theorem allows us to identify the space \(\mathcal{T}\) of all orientation-preserving isometries of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) with \(\mathbb {R}^2 \times \mathbb {R}/ 2 \pi \mathbb {Z}\).
Theorem 1.6

An orientation-preserving isometry of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) is represented uniquely as the composition \(T_v \circ R_\theta \) of a rotation followed by a translation. Consequently, this gives a bijection \(\iota \colon \mathbb {R}^2 \times \mathbb {R}/ 2 \pi \mathbb {Z}\rightarrow \mathcal{T}\) defined as \((v, \theta ) \mapsto T_v \circ R_\theta \).

Definition 1.7

For any orientation-preserving isometry \(f = T_v \circ R_\theta \) of \(\mathbb {R}^2\), call \(-\theta \) as the rotation angle of \(f\). Note the minus sign in front of \(\theta \). This is to measure the rotation positively in the clockwise direction.

We give the space \(\mathcal{I}\) of all orientation-preserving isometries of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) the topology of \(\mathbb {R}^2 \times \mathbb {R}/ 2 \pi \mathbb {Z}\) mapped under the bijection \(\iota \). In particular, the rotation angle is a continuous map on \(\mathcal{I}\).

Now we are ready to describe a sofa by its shape and movement.

Definition 1.8

A sofa \((S, f_t)\) is a nonempty, compact, connected subset \(S\) of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) called the shape of sofa equipped with a movement, which is a rigid motion that moves \(S\) from \(I_H\) to \(I_V\) inside \(L\). Formally speaking, the movement of sofa \(S\) is a sign-preserving isometry \(f_t\) of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) continuously parametrized by time \(0 \leq t \leq T\) for some \(T \geq 0\), so that it satisfies the following conditions.

  1. The moving sofa \(S_t = f_t(S)\) at any time \(0 \leq t \leq T\) is always a subset of \(L\).

  2. (Initial condition) The sofa \(S_0 = f_0(S)\) at the beginning of the movement is a subset of \(I_H\).

  3. (Final condition) The sofa \(S_T = f_T(S)\) at the end of the movement is a subset of \(I_V\).

Now the moving sofa problem can be formalized clearly.

Problem 1.9 Moving Sofa Problem

Does the maximum value of the area of the shape of a sofa exist? If so, which sofa attains such a maximum value?

We aim to prove that the answer is attained by Gerver’s sofa [ Ger92 ] .
Conjecture 1.10

Gerver’s sofa [ Ger92 ] attains the maximum possible area of a sofa.
