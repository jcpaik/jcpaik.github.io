1 Setup

In this section, we define the problem rigorously and introduce essential concepts for the proof. Thus, the definitions and notations introduced here will be used throughly for the rest of the document. We will also describe existing works on the moving sofa problem using terminologies introduced.

Definitions

Assume a fixed plane \(\mathbb {R}^2\) with the \(xy\)-coordinate system, so the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinate each refers to the first (horizontal) and second (vertical) coordinate respectively. First we set up the hallway.

Definition 1.1

The hallway \(L = I_H \cup I_V\) is the union of its horizontal side \(I_H = (-\infty , 1] \times [0, 1]\) and vertical side \(I_V = [0, 1] \times (-\infty , 1]\).

Define the horizontal strip \(H = \mathbb {R}\times [0, 1]\) and vertical strip \(V = [0, 1] \times \mathbb {R}\). With this, \(I_H = L \cap H\) and \(I_V = L \cap V\).

We want to move the sofa from the hallway’s horizontal side \(I_H\) to its vertical side \(I_V\). We also give names to the sides and vertices of the hallway \(L\) for future use. The convention is that the sides are in lowercase and the vertices are in boldcase lowercase. The names of sides follow that of [ Rom18 ] .
Definition 1.2

Let \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) and \(d\) be the sides

\begin{align*} a & = \{ 1\} \times (-\infty , 1] \qquad b = \{ 0\} \times (-\infty , 0] \\ c & = (-\infty , 1] \times \{ 1\} \qquad d = (-\infty , 0] \times \{ 0\} \end{align*}

of \(L\).

Definition 1.3

Let \(\mathbf{x}= (0, 0)\) and \(\mathbf{y}= (1, 1)\) be the inner and outer corner of the hallway \(L\) respectively.

We describe the movement by a continuum of sign-preserving isometries of \(\mathbb {R}^2\).

Definition 1.4

A sofa \((S, f_t)\) is a nonempty, compact, connected subset \(S\) of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) called the shape of sofa equipped with a movement, which is a rigid motion that moves \(S\) from \(I_H\) to \(I_V\) inside \(L\). Formally speaking, the movement of sofa \(S\) is a sign-preserving isometry \(f_t\) of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) continuously parametrized by time \(0 \leq t \leq T\) for some \(T \geq 0\), so that it satisfies the following conditions.

  1. The moving sofa \(S_t = f_t(S)\) at any time \(0 \leq t \leq T\) is always a subset of \(L\).

  2. (Initial condition) The sofa \(S_0 = f_0(S)\) at the beginning of the movement is a subset of \(I_H\).

  3. (Final condition) The sofa \(S_T = f_T(S)\) at the end of the movement is a subset of \(I_V\).

An important observation used by previous works [ Ger92 , Rom18 , KR18 ] is to look at the sofa movement in the frame of sofa and not the hallway. That is, instead of fixing the hallway \(L\) and moving the sofa \(S_t\) inside \(L\) over time \(t\), we follow the frame of reference of the sofa, making \(S\) stationary, and move the hallway \(L_t\) so that \(L_t\) always contains \(S\).

Definition 1.5 Alternative definition of a sofa

A sofa \((S, f_t)\) consists of the followings. First, a shape \(S\) which is a nonempty, compact, connected subset of \(\mathbb {R}^2\). Second, the hallway movement \(g_t = f_t^{-1}\) which is a sign-preserving isometry continuously parametrized over a time interval \(t \in [0, T]\) so that it satisfies the followings.

  1. The moving hallway \(L_t = g_t(L)\) at any time \(t \in [0, T]\) contains \(S\).

  2. At the beginning of the movement, the sofa \(S\) is contained inside \(g_0(\).

  3. At the end of the movement, the sofa \(S\) is contained inside \(g_T(I_V) = g_T(V) \cap L_T\), the vertical side of \(L_T\).

Without loss of generality, we can assume that \(S\) is already positioned in \(S_0\), so that \(g_0\) is the identity map of \(\mathbb {R}^2\), and thus \(L_0 = L\) and \(S\) is contained in \(H\) in particular. With such assumption, the three conditions of Definition 1.5 can be represented as the following single statement.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:inter} S \subseteq H \cap g_T(V) \cap \bigcap _{0 \leq t \leq T} L_t. \end{equation}
1.1

In this way, the sofa \(S\) is a connected subset of a connected component of the intersection in Equation 1.1. So the problem of maximizing the area of \(S\) becomes the problem of maximizing the area of the connected component of the intersection of two strips \(H\), \(g_T(V)\) and the rotating hallways \(L_t\).

We will use the following notations for translation/rotation map.

Definition 1.6

For any vector \(v \in \mathbb {R}^2\), let \(T_v\) be the translation map \(T_v(w) = w + v\) of the plane by \(v\). For any \(v \in \mathbb {R}^2\) and \(C \subset \mathbb {R}^2\), write \(T_v(C)\) as \(C + v\). For any angle \(\theta \in \mathbb {R}\), let \(R_\theta \) be the rotation map of \(\mathbb {R}^2\) that rotates the plane along the center of the plane with counterclockwise angle \(\theta \).

The following unit vectors will be useful in describing geometry of a general sofa.

Definition 1.7

For any \(t \in \mathbb {R}\), let \(u_t\) be the vector \((\cos t, \sin t)\) and \(v_t\) be the vector \((\sin t, -\cos t)\) so that \(u_t, v_t\) forms the frame rotated counterclockwise by an angle of \(t\).

History

The area of an explicit sofa gives a lower bound of the maximum sofa area. Since the problem was introduced, sofas of area \(2.2074\dots \) (by Hammersley [ Ham68 ] ), \(2.21563\dots \) (by C. Francis [ Guy77 ] ) and \(2.215649\dots \) (by R. Guy [ Guy77 ] ) were successively constructed over time. In 1997, Gerver [ Ger92 ] found the sofa of currently best known area \(2.2195\dots \) by considerations of the local maximality of a sofa. The boundary of Gerver’s sofa consists of 3 line segments and 15 analytic curves, making its derivation complicated. Romik [ Rom18 ] simplified the derivation of Gerver’s sofa in his work on ambidextrous sofa.

On the other hand, the current best known upper bound \(2.37\) of sofa area is found by Kallus and Romik in 2017 [ KR18 ] by a computer-assisted proof. It is conjectured that the Gerver’s sofa indeed attains the maximum sofa area, a speculation held by authors worked on this problem [ Ger92 , Rom18 ] that is supported experimentally by gradient descent computation on the problem’s polygonal variant [ Gib14 ] . Thus, under the assumption that Gerver’s sofa, the resolution of moving sofa problem boils down to improving the upper bound of possible sofa area.
