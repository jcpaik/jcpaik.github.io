2.1 Monotone Movement

By Lemma 1.12, we can restrict our attention to sofas that rotate by an angle of \(\beta \in [0, \pi / 2]\). Such an arbitrary sofa \(S\) can be moved in the following way we name as a monotone movement. First, move a copy of \(S_0\) inside \(L\) in the positive \(x\) and \(y\) direction until it hits both sides \(a\) and \(c\) of the hallway \(L\). Then, for every moment \(t \in [0, \beta ]\), we rotate the sofa by the unit angular speed while keeping it touching the sides \(a\) and \(c\). This way, the sofa \(S_t\) is parametrized by the angle it is rotated during movement. Such a movement is used by previous works [ Ger92 , Rom18 , KR18 ] . The rest of this subsection defines the monotone movement and justifies that it is a valid movement.

First we introduce some definitions before describing the monotone movement. We will use the following unit vectors to describe the geometry of a general sofa.

Definition 2.1

For any \(t \in \mathbb {R}\), let \(u_t\) be the vector \((\cos t, \sin t)\) and \(v_t\) be the vector \((\sin t, -\cos t)\) so that \(u_t, v_t\) forms the frame rotated counterclockwise by an angle of \(t\).

In particular, we define the support functions \(p_S, q_S\) of a sofa \(S\) as the following.
Definition 2.2

For any \(t \in \mathbb {R}\) and a closed, compact set \(S \subset \mathbb {R}^2\), define the support functions \(p_S, q_S \colon \mathbb {R}\rightarrow \mathbb {R}\) as

\[ p_S(t) = \max _{x \in S} x \cdot u_t \]

and

\[ q_S(t) = \max _{x \in S} x \cdot v_t. \]

If the sofa \(S\) is clear from the context, we omit the subscript and denote the support functions as \(p\) and \(q\).

Theorem 2.3

For any closed, compact \(S \subset \mathbb {R}^2\), the support functions \(p_S\) and \(q_S\) are continuous.

We will use the following definition often as well.

Definition 2.4

Define the quadrants \(Q^+ = (-\infty , 1] \times (-\infty , 1]\) and \(Q^- = (-\infty , 0) \times (-\infty , 0)\). With this, \(L\) can be expressed as \(Q^+ \setminus Q^-\).

Now we show that any sofa with rotation angle in between \(0\) and \(\pi / 2\) can be repositioned to have the following monotone movement.

Definition 2.5

A sofa \((S, f_t)\) with hallway movement \(g_t = f_t^{-1}\) and rotation angle \(\beta \in [0, \pi / 2]\) is said to have a monotone movement if it satisfies the following.

  • The sofa movement \(f_t\) is parametrized by angles \(t \in [0, \beta ]\).

  • \(q_{S}(0) = p_{S}(\beta ) = 1\). That is, \(S\) touches the sides \(c(0) = g_0(c)\) and \(a(\beta ) = g_\beta (a)\) of \(L_0\) and \(L_\beta \) respectively.

  • \(g_{t} = R_t \circ T_{(p_{S}(t) -1, q_{S}(t) -1)}\). That is, the hallway \(L_{t} = g_t(L)\) at time \(t\) is \(L\) rotated counterclockwise by an angle of \(t\) and translated to touch \(S\) on the sides \(a(t)\) and \(c(t)\).

Theorem 2.6

Let \((S', f'_t)\) be any sofa with shape \(S'\), movement \(f'_t\) and a rotation angle of \(\beta \in [0, \pi / 2]\). Then we can reposition \(S'\) to a sofa shape \(S\) (that is, there is an orientation-preserving isometry \(m\) so that \(S = m(S')\)) with sofa movement \(f_t\) and hallway movement \(g_t = f_t^{-1}\) parametrized by \(t \in [0, \beta ]\) which is monotone (Definition 2.5).

Proof ▼

First we set up the shape \(S\) of the new sofa. Geometrically, \(S\) is the translation of \(S'_0\) that touches the upper and right sides of \(P = H \cap R_\beta (V)\) inside. Note that \(P\) is a parallelogram with lower left corner \((0, 0)\) when \(\beta \in [0, \pi / 2)\), and \(P\) is the horizontal strip \(H\) when \(\beta = \pi / 2\). The following paragraph is merely a formalization of this step.

Define \(S\) as the translation of \(S'_0 = f'_0(S')\) by a vector \(v \in \mathbb {R}^2\) that satisfies \(q_{S}(0) = p_{S}(\beta ) = 1\). The condition for \(v\) to satisfy \(q_{S}(0) = p_{S}(\beta ) = 1\) are the followings.

\begin{gather*} q_{S}(0) = q_{S'_0}(0) + v \cdot v_0 = 1\\ p_{S}(\beta ) = p_{S'_0}(\beta ) + v \cdot u_\beta = 1 \end{gather*}

These are linear conditions of \(v\), and such a \(v\) exists uniquely for every \(\beta \in [0, \pi / 2)\) as \(v_0\) and \(u_\beta \) forms a basis of \(\mathbb {R}^2\). For \(\beta = \pi / 2\), the two equations overlap as \(p_S(\pi / 2) = q_S(0)\) for any sofa \(S\) by definition, so take any solution of \(v\). Now by letting \(m = T_v \circ f'_0\) and sending the sofa \((S', f'_t)\) under \(m\), we have a sofa of shape \(S = m(S')\) and movement \(\tilde{f}_t = f'_t \circ m^{-1}\), so that \(\tilde{f}_t(S) = f'_t(S') = S'_t\). That is, the movement \(\tilde{f}_t\) of \(S\) parametrized by \(t \in [0, T]\) follows the exact same movement of \(S'\). Also, we get \(q_S(0) = p_S(\beta ) = 1\) by how we chose \(v\).

Now we show that the sofa shape \(S\) with movement \(f_t = g_t^{-1}\) defined as \(g_t = R_t \circ T_{(p_{S}(t) -1, q_{S}(t) -1)}\) over \(t \in [0, \beta ]\) gives a valid sofa. First, observe that \(f_t\) is continuously parametrized by \(t\) because \(p_S(t)\) and \(q_S(t)\) are continuous (Theorem 2.3). It remains for us to show that

  • \(S\) is contained in \(g_0(H)\)

  • \(S\) is contained in \(L_t = g_t(L)\) for all \(t \in [0, \beta ]\)

  • \(S\) is contained in \(g_\beta (V)\)

by the moving hallway definition of a sofa (Definition 1.13). First we show the second item \(S \subseteq L_t\).

Before showing \(S \subseteq L_t\), we observe the following. The initial map \(\tilde{f}_0\) of the movement \(\tilde{f}\) is \(T_{-v}\), a translation. So it has a rotation angle of \(0\). As \(\tilde{f}_t\) has the same rotation angle \(\beta \) as the original movement \(f'_t\), the final map \(\tilde{f}_T\) of the movement \(\tilde{f}\) has a rotation angle of \(\beta \). Because the rotation angle of \(\tilde{f}_{t'}\) is continuous respect to \(t' \in [0, T]\), by the intermediate value theorem, for every \(\theta \in [0, \beta ]\) we have some \(t' \in [0, T]\) such that \(\tilde{f}_{t'}\) has a rotation angle of \(\theta \).

Now we take arbitrary \(t \in [0, \beta ]\) and show \(S \subseteq L_t\). We can find some \(t \in [0, T]\) such that \(\tilde{f}_t\) has a rotation angle of \(\theta \) by the previous paragraph. So the moving hallway \(\tilde{L} = \tilde{f}_t^{-1}(L)\) at time \(t\) is rotated counterclockwise from \(L\) by \(t\). Geometrically, \(L_t\) is a copy of \(\tilde{L}\) translated in the directions \(-u_\theta \) and \(-v_\theta \) until it hits the sofa \(S\).
